About

NANOFAB FOR STUDENTS,
BY STUDENTS.

CWRU Hacker Fab is a student-run club making semiconductor fabrication hands-on and accessible at Case Western Reserve University.

We build our own photolithography, thin-film, and vacuum tools from open-source designs developed at Carnegie Mellon — giving undergraduates real experience with the equipment normally locked away in graduate research labs.

Everything we build is documented and shared openly with the growing Hacker Fab network at CMU, Ohio State, Waterloo, UC Irvine, and MIT. Our long-term goal is to fabricate a working MOSFET entirely in-house.

01Training & Workshops

Semester-long lecture series in lithography, thin-film, and semiconductor devices. Tool certification and EHS safety training for all members.

02Project-Based Learning

Students actually build and troubleshoot real fab equipment. Hands-on projects at the MORE Center for thin-film metrology and characterization.

03Community & Collaboration

Open-source documentation, interdisciplinary teams across MSE, ECE, PHYS, and MAE, and a growing industry speaker series.

Phase 1 · Active · $2,500 Funded via Think[box]
UV Lithography Stepper
A student-assembled UV lithography system based on Carnegie Mellon's open-source design. Uses a repurposed projector lens and UV LED to expose photoresist-coated wafers through a chrome-on-glass mask, achieving 2 micron feature resolution.
Why This Matters
Photolithography is the core process of semiconductor manufacturing — every chip ever made relies on it. Without the ability to pattern features on a surface, nothing else in the fab works. This is our most important build, and the one that makes every downstream project possible.
What This Unlocks
→2µm resolution patterning — sufficient for MOSFET gate definition
→Foundation for all Phase 1–3 processes: deposition, etching, contacts
→Hands-on understanding of optics, alignment, and process control
→Replicable, documented design shared to the global Hacker Fab network
In Progress
CMU Reference: $3,015  ·  Our Build: $2,500

Phase 1 · Under Construction
Vacuum Spin Coater
A student-designed vacuum spin coater that spins wafers at controlled speeds using a brushless motor and vacuum chuck, depositing a thin, uniform photoresist layer before UV exposure.
Why This Matters
Before any wafer can be patterned it needs an even coat of photoresist. A non-uniform layer causes inconsistent exposure and failed devices. This is step zero of every process we'll ever run — no spin coater, no lithography.
What This Unlocks
→Uniform resist deposition — prerequisite for every lithography step
→Training in thin-film uniformity, spin parameters, and process control
→Completes the full patterning pipeline alongside the UV stepper
Under Construction
Build Cost: ~$200 (DIY)

Phase 1 · Planning
Electroless Plating System
A wet chemistry system that deposits metal films onto patterned surfaces using an autocatalytic chemical reaction — no vacuum, no high voltages, no expensive equipment.
Why This Matters
Metal contacts are required on virtually every semiconductor device. Electroless plating lets us add metal to patterned wafers at a fraction of the cost of vacuum evaporation, and teaches students real wet-chemistry and surface science in the process.
What This Unlocks
→Metal films for test structures, resistors, and simple device contacts
→Low-cost bridge from lithography to device-level work within Phase 1
→Hands-on experience with surface chemistry and thin-film metallization
Planning Phase

Phase 2 · Future Build
Thermal Evaporator
A vacuum chamber system that resistively heats a source metal until it evaporates and condenses on the substrate, depositing ultra-thin, high-purity metal films used for transistor contacts.
Why This Matters
Clean, controlled metal contacts are essential for any working transistor. Thermal evaporation produces higher-quality films than electroless plating and is the standard method in research fabs worldwide. Adding this moves us from simple test structures to devices we can actually measure.
What This Unlocks
→High-purity metal contacts (Al, Au, Cr) for source, drain, and gate electrodes
→First step toward full multi-layer device fabrication in Phase 3
→Student experience with vacuum technology and deposition rate control
Future Phase

Phase 2 · Future Build
RF Sputtering System
An RF-powered vacuum system that ejects atoms from a target material to coat the substrate. Unlike thermal evaporation, it can deposit non-conducting materials like silicon dioxide — the gate oxide critical to a MOSFET.
Why This Matters
A MOSFET needs a thin insulating gate oxide between the gate electrode and the semiconductor channel. Only RF sputtering can deposit this layer. This is the capability that unlocks Phase 3 — without it, we can't complete a transistor.
What This Unlocks
→Gate oxide (SiO₂) deposition — the defining layer of a MOSFET gate stack
→Dielectric layers for capacitors and device passivation
→Combined metal + insulator stacks needed for full device integration
Future Phase
CMU Reference: ~$18,400 (DIY build)

Long-Term Goal · Year 3+
MOSFET Fabrication
A working Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor fabricated entirely in-house by CWRU students. The MOSFET is the building block of every modern computer chip — and building one from scratch requires mastering every process in our roadmap.
Why This Is the Goal
The MOSFET is the most important electronic device of the modern era — the building block of every processor, memory chip, and amplifier. A student-fabricated MOSFET proves that the entire pipeline works. It is the most meaningful benchmark for a student-run fab and a capstone that will define what CWRU Hacker Fab means.
What Getting Here Requires
→UV lithography (Phase 1) for gate, source, and drain patterning
→RF sputtering (Phase 2) for gate oxide deposition
→Thermal evaporation (Phase 2) for metal contacts
→Tube furnace (Phase 2) for dopant activation and oxidation
→Probe station (Phase 3) for I–V electrical characterization
The North Star
Multi-year roadmap · Requires all phases
